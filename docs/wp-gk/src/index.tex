\begin{abstract}
\greekfi is a protocol that enables decentralized, fully collateralized options
in three settlement modes: American non-settled (oracle-free), American oracle-settled,
and European oracle-settled.
It is designed for composability, exercisability, and universal use
on any EVM-compatible chain, and allows any ERC20 token to be used as
collateral and consideration, including wBTC, wETH, stETH, USDC, and USDT.

The protocol pairs these primitives with a live RFQ-driven trading venue,
per-holder cash- or in-kind settlement via injectable swapper contracts,
and a modified-Morpho vault for capital-efficient option writing, lending, and yield collection.
This composability enables an options ecosystem that goes beyond
traditional financial strategies while remaining oracle-optional at the option-token layer.

\end{abstract}

\section{Protocol Overview}
\paragraph*{Protocol Summary -}
The protocol uses two coupled ERC20 tokens to represent an option position: an Option Token and a Receipt Token. As an American option, the Option Token can be exercised at any time before expiration and Receipt Tokens allow redemption after expiration. 

\subsection{Protocol Details}
Options are represented by ERC20 Option Tokens (OT) that allow a user to excercise at specific strike price of N units of consideration for a specific underlying token, before an expiration date (Consideration is typically a cash asset like USDC, and Collateral is typically an asset like wETH).
This OT is unique, and temporary as it has an expiration date, after which it is worthless. 

To write an Option, we mint an OT by collateralizing assets which also results in a Receipt Token (RT) with the OT. 
The RT, also an ERC20, represents the short option position and redeemability of the asset that was collateralized, which can happen after expiration.
Also, the reverse of minting, combining the OT with the RT, also redeems Collateral.
Both tokens can be swap and delivered through DEXs/AMMs and RFQs on-chain.

The creation of the contract pair (OT + RT) is done throught a factory contract, which allows for upgradability of the protocol. 
This also helps in managing all the contracts created as a source of truth.
Each OT+RT pair is represented by a tuple of the following parameters:

\subsection{Settlement}

The protocol supports three settlement modes, fixed at option creation:

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|p{2.4cm} p{2.0cm} p{2.4cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Mode} & \textbf{Oracle} & \textbf{Pre-expiry exercise} \\
\hline
American non-settled & none & yes (pay consideration) \\
\hline
American settled & required & yes (pay consideration) \\
\hline
European & required & disabled (claim post-expiry only) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\caption{Settlement modes. The mode is determined by the \texttt{(oracle, isEuro)} pair at creation.}
\label{tab:modes}
\end{table}

In oracle-settled modes (American settled and European), once expired, any caller
runs a permissionless \texttt{settle()} which latches the spot price from the configured oracle
(Chainlink, Uniswap v3 TWAP, or any \texttt{IPriceOracle} implementation). The contract
records whether the option finished in-the-money (ITM) and reserves the appropriate
slice of collateral for option-holder claims; the rest flows to the short side.

ITM holders then call \texttt{claim()} to retrieve their payout. The default settlement
asset is the consideration (cash); a holder may opt into in-kind collateral by calling
\texttt{requestCollateral()} before the post-expiry swap runs. A one-shot
\texttt{convertResidualToConsideration(swapper, minOut, routeHint)} call sweeps the
non-opt-in slice of collateral into consideration through an injectable
\texttt{ISettlementSwapper} -- typically a thin wrapper around Uniswap v4's flash
accounting, the Universal Router, or an aggregator like 1inch. The protocol itself
remains DEX-agnostic.

European options use the same \texttt{claim()} mechanism but disable pre-expiry
\texttt{exercise()}, which makes them economically equivalent to a cash-settled European
option even when the holder opts for in-kind delivery.

\subsection{Capital Efficiency via the \greekfi Vault}

To achieve capital efficiency, less collateral must support the same exposure.
Traditional clearing houses do this through margining; in DeFi, lending markets
play the same role. We bundle the lending pattern into a modified-Morpho vault
shipped alongside the protocol, so writers do not need to operate it manually.

The basic mechanic. To mint a 1 wETH option, the writer supplies only $\alpha$ wETH
of their own (e.g. 0.3 wETH) and the vault borrows the remaining $1 - \alpha$
(e.g. 0.7 wETH) against the freshly-minted Receipt Token (RT). The vault then
sells the Option Token (OT) into the live RFQ market for premium. The position
nets to: writer owns the premium plus a leveraged short-volatility exposure;
the lender holds RT as loan collateral.

To unwind, the OT is bought back, paired with the RT, and redeemed for the underlying
wETH; the loan is repaid. If the OT moves against the writer and the LTV crosses
its threshold, the lender liquidates by buying the matching OT, redeeming the
collateral, and returning the residual to the writer. The LTV ceiling is a function
of strike and current spot.

The vault also collects yield: short-side holders earn premium directly, while the
RT held as loan collateral compounds via the lending leg. The same primitive supports
covered-call, covered-put, and put-spread strategies without bespoke contracts per
strategy -- the vault simply parameterises which option series to write each cycle.

\section{Applications}

\paragraph*{Trading (live)}
The \texttt{/trade} page is live and integrates with Bebop and 0x RFQ.
Traditional constant-product AMMs ($xy=k$) cannot price the low-volume, asymmetric
distributions that options generate, so the protocol routes order flow through RFQ
market makers who quote signed prices on demand. OTs can also be auto-minted inside
\texttt{transfer()} for seamless OTC delivery, allowing market makers to fill an
order without holding inventory ahead of time.

\paragraph*{Cash Settlement (live)}
At expiry, the protocol's per-holder settlement flag and the
\texttt{convertResidualToConsideration} sweep allow ITM holders to receive USDC
(or any consideration token) directly, rather than the in-kind collateral residual.
The on-chain swap is delegated to an \texttt{ISettlementSwapper} -- shipped today as
either a Uniswap v4 flash-accounting wrapper (\texttt{V4SettlementSwapper}, where the
borrow-A-pay-B semantics let the swap atomically borrow consideration and repay in
the freshly-released collateral), or a Universal Router wrapper
(\texttt{UniversalRouterSwapper}, which delegates routing to Uniswap's auto-router and
covers v2/v3/v4 in one call). Holders who prefer in-kind collateral opt out via
\texttt{requestCollateral()} before the sweep runs. The protocol stays DEX-agnostic;
new swappers (1inch, 0x, Curve, RFQ feeds) can be added without protocol changes.

\paragraph*{Default Swaps}
The protocol provides default swaps as insurance for risk management in debt markets.
An oracle or third party can \textbf{unlock} the OT to allow exercise on the
default event.

\paragraph*{Yield, Vault, and Lending (live)}
The \texttt{/yield} page lets users write covered calls and puts directly, with the
modified-Morpho vault handling the borrow-against-RT leg automatically. Premium
flows back to the writer; lenders earn against the RT-as-collateral loan; market
makers price the OT through the same RFQ feed used by \texttt{/trade}. The strategy
is the on-chain analogue of XYLD-style covered-call ETFs, but composable -- the
resulting vault share is itself an ERC20 that downstream protocols can integrate.

\paragraph*{Crosschain Interoperability}
With protocols such as LayerZero and Hop, crosschain interoperability is increasingly
practical. The system can be designed to be interoperable across chains, with careful
handling of the four moving parts (Collateral, Consideration, OT, RT) per chain.
The factory contract can be upgraded to support crosschain option creation.

\paragraph*{Option Drops Instead of Token Drops}
In TradFi, startup employees vest options and exercise for tax purposes. A similar
mechanic on-chain provides option drops as an alternative to token drops, distributing
upside without giving up immediate sell-pressure protection.

\paragraph*{Compound Options}
A compound option is an option on an option -- a second derivative. The protocol
handles this natively: nothing prevents the OT or RT of one option from being used
as collateral for another option.
